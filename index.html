Crypto — highest risk, watch closely

Crypto is far more volatile and speculative than stocks — a coin can lose most of its value fast, and unlike a company it has no earnings, assets, or dividends behind it. The same scores and price badges apply, but expect very low steadiness scores — that's honest, not a glitch. Only ever put in money you could afford to lose entirely.

Glossary — what the words mean

200-day averageThe average closing price over the last ~10 months. A standard line for "is this stock generally rising or falling?"
vs 200-day avgHow far today's price sits above or below that line, as a percentage.
Extended runPrice is more than ~15% above its 200-day average — it has risen far and fast. Can mean real strength, or that it's stretched and due for a rest.
Deep downtrendPrice is more than ~15% below its 200-day average — a sustained fall.
Aegis ScoreA 0–100 grade averaging four factors below. It rewards calm, steady stocks over hype — and is not a prediction the price will rise.
Score bandsVery steady (85+), Solid (70–84), Mixed (55–69), Shaky (40–54), Rough (below 40).
Trend (factor)Is the price above its long-run average — i.e. generally rising?
Momentum (factor)How much the price has climbed or fallen over the past year.
Stability (factor)How calm or wild the price swings are (its volatility). Calmer scores higher; a broad fund is about 15%/yr.
Resilience (factor)How deep its worst fall was this year. A shallower drop scores higher.
Price badgeReads whether a stock is a good price right now: BUY · Good price (cheap / on sale), FAIR price (middling), PRICEY · Getting pricey (run up a bit), PRICEY · Expensive (run up a lot). Based on the trader gauges below.
RSIRelative Strength Index (0–100). A speedometer for price. Under 30 = it's been beaten down and may be cheap; over 70 = it's run up fast and may be expensive. (Traders call these "oversold" and "overbought.")
52-week rangeWhere today's price sits between its lowest (0%) and highest (100%) of the past year. Near the low end is the cheaper end.
Bollinger Bands / %BA stock's normal price range (its 20-day average, plus/minus its usual swing). %B under 0 = unusually cheap vs recent range; over 100 = unusually stretched.
Falling knifeA stock that's cheap and still falling in a downtrend. "Cheap" alone isn't a buy — a healthy stock on a dip is a far better entry than one in free-fall.
SignalThe trend rule's current call: BUY (just rose above the line), HOLD (above it), STAY OUT (below it — wait in cash), SELL (just dropped below it).
BacktestRunning a buy/sell rule over 10+ years of real history to see how it actually would have performed — evidence, not a guess.
Buy & holdBuying once and never selling. The simple benchmark every strategy is measured against.
Drawdown / worst dropThe biggest fall from a high point down to the next low. A gut-check on how much fear you'd have to sit through.
Win rateThe share of completed buy-then-sell trades that ended in a profit.
In marketThe share of days a rule was actually invested, versus sitting safely in cash.
Fee (expense ratio)A fund's yearly cost as a % of your money, charged automatically. Lower is better; over decades it adds up.
CompoundingGrowth earning its own growth over time — the quiet engine behind long-term wealth.
Company fundamentalsNumbers about the underlying business — how profitable it is, how fast it's growing, how much it owes. They describe the company, not a prediction of the stock price.
P/E ratioPrice-to-earnings: the share price divided by yearly profit per share — roughly how many years of current profits you're paying for. Lower can mean cheaper; a very low P/E can also flag a troubled business.
Forward P/EThe same idea as P/E, but using analysts' forecast of next year's profits. A forward P/E lower than the normal one means profits are expected to grow.
Profit marginWhat share of every sales dollar becomes actual profit. Higher usually means a more efficient, higher-quality business.
Revenue growthHow much yearly sales rose versus a year ago. Positive and steady is the healthy sign.
Return on equity (ROE)Profit earned on the money shareholders have in the business. Higher is generally better — though very high can come from heavy borrowing.
Dividend yieldYearly cash paid to shareholders as a % of the share price. 0% means the company reinvests everything instead of paying you cash.
Debt / equityHow much the company borrows versus its own capital. Higher means more debt and more risk if business slows. (Banks are left blank — the ratio doesn't mean the same for them.)
Market capThe whole company's value — share price times every share. Bigger companies are usually steadier; smaller ones swing more.
Analyst consensusThe average buy/hold/sell call from Wall Street analysts. A rough sentiment gauge, not a guarantee — the crowd is often wrong.
